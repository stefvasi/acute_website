About

Engineering at the edge of perception

ACUTE — ACoUstic and Tactile Engineering — is a research group at the University of Iceland's Faculty of Industrial Engineering, Mechanical Engineering and Computer Science. We work at the intersection of human perception science, engineering design, and applied acoustics.

Our research spans three connected pillars: vibrotactile interfaces that restore or augment sensation through touch; spatial audio and head-related transfer functions that personalise the listening experience for every individual; and wave-based room acoustics simulation that makes physically accurate modelling fast enough for real engineering use.

We collaborate with prosthetics manufacturer Össur, clinical partners at Landspítali — National University Hospital, and acoustic software company Treble Technologies. Our work is funded by RANNÍS, NordForsk, and the European Union.

Research Areas

01Haptics

Vibrotactile Perception

We systematically map the perceptual limits of the human sense of touch as it responds to vibration — how sensitive is the skin at different frequencies, at different body sites, and under different conditions?

Key finding: presenting vibrotactile patterns sequentially yields dramatically higher recognition accuracy than simultaneous presentation. This principle drives the design of every feedback system we build.

93%Sequential recognition accuracy
26%Simultaneous accuracy
Selected publications
Ævarsson et al. (2022). Vibrotactile threshold measurements at the wrist. ACM Transactions on Applied Perception.
Yeganeh et al. (2024). Sequential vs. simultaneous vibrotactile stimulation on the forearm. Applied Sciences.
Yeganeh et al. (2023). Effects of stimulus frequency and location on vibrotactile perception. Actuators.
02Assistive Technology

Prosthetic Haptic Feedback

Transfemoral amputees lose the sensory feedback that tells them where their foot is and how it interacts with the ground. We design forearm-based vibrotactile systems that restore this information in real time.

Conducted in direct collaboration with Össur ehf. and physiotherapy researchers at the University of Iceland's School of Health Sciences and Landspítali — National University Hospital.

72%Sequential recognition (prosthetic context)
Selected publications
Karimi et al. (2026). Forearm-based vibrotactile interface for transfemoral prosthetic feedback. Biomimetics.
Karimi et al. (2025). Haptic feedback systems for lower-limb prosthetics: A systematic review. Bioengineering.
03Assistive Technology

Sensory Substitution

We develop vibrotactile and audio-haptic devices that translate visual or auditory information into touch patterns. Our cochlear implant project encodes rhythmic and low-frequency musical features into tactile patterns, providing an additional sensory channel for over half a million CI users worldwide.

Selected publications
Kristjánsson et al. (2026). Sensory substitution through vibrotactile stimulation. Springer.
Yeganeh et al. (2022). Vibrotactile sleeve to improve music enjoyment of cochlear implant users. ASME IMECE.
04Acoustics

Spatial Audio & HRTFs

We manufacture silicone pinna replicas with systematically varied geometry, measuring their HRTFs in an anechoic chamber to isolate individual acoustic contributions. Our open Viking HRTF Dataset v2 is used by researchers worldwide.

We also apply machine learning to predict HRTFs from anthropometric measurements, working toward personalised spatial audio from a photograph. Conducted in collaboration with Treble Technologies.

Selected publications
Sumner, Riedel & Unnthorsson (2025). Design and manufacture of synthetic pinnæ for HRTF measurement. Cogent Engineering.
Fernández et al. (2023). Prediction of head-related transfer functions using machine learning. Acoustics.
Spagnol, Miccini & Unnthorsson (2020). The Viking HRTF Dataset v2. Zenodo.
05Computational Acoustics

Room Acoustics Simulation

We develop structure-preserving model order reduction techniques that compress the wave physics into a low-dimensional approximation, achieving physically stable solutions at a fraction of the computational cost. Conducted in collaboration with Treble Technologies, funded by RANNÍS.

100×Speedup over full-order model
Selected publications
Bonthu et al. (2026). Stable model order reduction for time-domain room acoustics. International Journal for Numerical Methods in Engineering.
06Perception Science

Attention, Foraging & Haptic Illusions

We study human foraging — the sequential collection of multiple targets — and ask what role synchrony, cross-modal cues, and vibrotactile signals play in directing attention.

We also investigate haptic illusions that reveal the brain's assumptions about touch. In the intensity order illusion, a strong vibration followed by a weak one causes the second to feel as if it arose above the first. Such illusions have direct consequences for haptic display design.

Selected publications
Makarov et al. (2024). Visual and auditory synchrony effects in human foraging. Attention, Perception & Psychophysics.
Hoffmann et al. (2019). The intensity order illusion in vibrotactile perception. Journal of Neurophysiology.
