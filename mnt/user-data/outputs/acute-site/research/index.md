      <div>
        <h2 class="area-title">Vibrotactile Perception</h2>
        <div class="area-body">
          <p>We systematically map the perceptual limits of the human sense of touch as it responds to vibration — how sensitive is the skin at different frequencies, at different body sites, and under different conditions? These fundamentals constrain every design decision in wearable haptic devices.</p>
          <p>Key finding: presenting vibrotactile patterns <em>sequentially</em> — one actuator at a time — yields dramatically higher recognition accuracy than simultaneous presentation. This principle drives the design of every feedback system we build.</p>
        </div>

        <h2 class="area-title">Prosthetic Haptic Feedback</h2>
        <div class="area-body">
          <p>Transfemoral amputees lose the sensory feedback that tells them where their foot is and how it interacts with the ground. We design and evaluate forearm-based vibrotactile systems that restore this information in real time, routing signals from prosthetic sensors to a sleeve worn on the residual arm.</p>
          <p>This work is conducted in direct collaboration with prosthetics manufacturer <strong>Össur ehf.</strong> and physiotherapy researchers at the University of Iceland's School of Health Sciences and <strong>Landspítali — National University Hospital</strong>.</p>

        <h2 class="area-title">Sensory Substitution</h2>
        <div class="area-body">
          <p>When one sense is unavailable, another can carry some of its load — if the substitution device is designed around the receiving channel's real capabilities. We develop vibrotactile and audio-haptic devices that translate visual or auditory information into touch patterns.</p>
          <p>Our cochlear implant project encodes rhythmic and low-frequency musical features into tactile patterns on the arm, providing an additional sensory channel. Over half a million cochlear implant users worldwide stand to benefit from this approach.</p>

      <div>
        <h2 class="area-title">Spatial Audio &amp; HRTFs</h2>
        <div class="area-body">
          <p>The outer ear (pinna) colours incoming sound differently depending on direction, giving the brain its cues for 3D sound localisation. Reproducing this in headphones requires knowing each listener's unique Head-Related Transfer Function (HRTF).</p>
          <p>We manufacture silicone pinna replicas with systematically varied geometry using 3D scanning and printing, then measure their HRTFs in our anechoic chamber — isolating the acoustic contribution of individual features. Our open <strong>Viking HRTF Dataset v2</strong> is used by researchers worldwide. This work is conducted in collaboration with <strong>Treble Technologies</strong>.</p>
        </div>

        <h2 class="area-title">Room Acoustics Simulation</h2>
        <div class="area-body">
          <p>Wave-based room acoustics simulation captures every reflection, diffraction, and absorption event in a space — but solving the underlying physics across millions of points in space and time is computationally prohibitive for parametric design studies.</p>
          <p>We develop structure-preserving model order reduction techniques that compress the physics into a low-dimensional approximation, achieving physically stable solutions at a fraction of the computational cost. This work is done in collaboration with <strong>Treble Technologies</strong> and funded by RANNÍS.</p>
        </div>

        <h2 class="area-title">Attention, Foraging &amp; Haptic Illusions</h2>
        <div class="area-body">
          <p>How does the brain decide what to notice in a cluttered environment? We study human foraging — the sequential collection of multiple targets from a visual scene — and ask what role synchrony, cross-modal cues, and vibrotactile signals play in directing attention.</p>
          <p>We also investigate haptic illusions that reveal the brain's assumptions about touch. In the <em>intensity order illusion</em>, presenting a strong vibration followed by a weak one causes the second to feel as if it arose from above the first — even when it didn't. Illusions like this have direct consequences for the design of any haptic information display.</p>
        </div>
