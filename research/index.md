<!-- 01 — Vibrotactile Perception -->
<div class="ra">
  <div class="ra__img">
    <img src="{{ '/images/lab_equipment.jpg' | relative_url }}" alt="Forearm sleeve with vibrotactile actuator array">
  </div>
  <div class="ra__txt">
    <div class="ra__num">01</div>
    <h2>Vibrotactile Perception</h2>
    <p>We map the perceptual limits of touch in response to vibration. Maximum sensitivity at the wrist peaks at 200 Hz on the inner surface; individual thresholds vary enormously, requiring personalised calibration.</p>
    <p>Presenting patterns <em>sequentially</em> yields <strong>93% recognition accuracy</strong>, versus 26% with simultaneous stimulation.</p>
    <div class="ra__pubs">
      <p class="ra__pubs-label">Key publications</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Yeganeh et al. (2024). Sequential vs. simultaneous stimulation. <em>Applied Sciences</em>. <a href="https://doi.org/10.3390/app14010043" target="_blank">doi:10.3390/app14010043</a></li>
        <li>Yeganeh et al. (2023). Frequency &amp; location on the forearm. <em>Actuators</em>. <a href="https://doi.org/10.3390/act12060224" target="_blank">doi:10.3390/act12060224</a></li>

<!-- 02 — Prosthetic Haptic Feedback -->
<div class="ra ra--flip">
  <div class="ra__img">

  <div class="ra__txt">
    <div class="ra__num">02</div>
    <h2>Prosthetic Haptic Feedback</h2>
    <p>We design forearm-based vibrotactile systems that route signals from prosthetic sensors to a wearable sleeve, restoring a proxy sense of foot position for transfemoral amputees. Sequential patterns achieve <strong>72% recognition accuracy</strong> — nearly double the 43% with simultaneous patterns.</p>
    <p>Developed with <strong>Össur ehf.</strong> and <strong>Landspítali</strong>.</p>
    <div class="ra__pubs">
      <p class="ra__pubs-label">Key publications</p>

    <h2>Sensory Substitution</h2>
    <p>We build vibrotactile and audio-haptic devices that translate visual or auditory information into touch, enabling impaired users to access information they would otherwise miss.</p>
    <p>Our cochlear implant project encodes musical rhythms into tactile patterns, reaching <strong>500,000+ CI users</strong> worldwide.</p>

<!-- 04 — Spatial Audio & HRTFs -->
<div class="ra ra--flip">
  <div class="ra__img">

    <div class="ra__num">04</div>
    <h2>Spatial Audio &amp; HRTFs</h2>
    <p>We manufacture silicone pinna replicas with systematically varied geometry, altering one feature at a time to isolate its acoustic contribution. We also train machine learning models to predict HRTFs from ear photographs.</p>
    <p>Our open <strong>Viking HRTF Dataset v2</strong> — 1513 positions, 20 pinna replicas — is used by researchers worldwide.</p>
    <div class="ra__pubs">
      <p class="ra__pubs-label">Key publications</p>

<!-- 06 — Attention, Foraging & Haptic Illusions -->
<div class="ra ra--flip">
  <div class="ra__img">
    <img src="{{ '/images/scan_pattern.png' | relative_url }}" alt="Foraging task display from attention study">
  </div>
  <div class="ra__txt">
    <div class="ra__num">06</div>
    <h2>Attention, Foraging &amp; Haptic Illusions</h2>
    <p>We study how synchrony, cross-modal cues, and vibrotactile signals guide human multi-target search. Visual synchrony alone dramatically cuts foraging time; a wrist vibration can be nearly as effective as an auditory cue.</p>
    <p>We have also documented the <strong>haptic intensity order illusion</strong>: strong-then-weak vibration order makes the second stimulus feel displaced — overriding the actual direction.</p>
    <div class="ra__pubs">
      <p class="ra__pubs-label">Key publications</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Makarov et al. (2024). Visual &amp; auditory synchrony in foraging. <em>Attention, Perception, &amp; Psychophysics</em>. <a href="https://doi.org/10.3758/s13414-023-02840-z" target="_blank">doi:10.3758/s13414-023-02840-z</a></li>
        <li>Hoffmann et al. (2019). The intensity order illusion. <em>Journal of Neurophysiology</em>. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1152/jn.00125.2019" target="_blank">doi:10.1152/jn.00125.2019</a></li>
